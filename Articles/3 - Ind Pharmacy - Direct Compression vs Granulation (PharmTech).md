## Overview

Three different processing technologies are used to manufacture tablets: direct compression, dry granulation, and wet granulation. Each has distinct process steps, equipment requirements, and suitability depending on the drug's physical and chemical properties.

## Direct Compression

### Process
Direct compression is the most straightforward method. All ingredients — the API and excipients — are blended together and then compressed directly into tablets. No granulation or drying is needed.

Steps:
1. Blend API with directly compressible excipients (e.g., microcrystalline cellulose, spray-dried lactose)
2. Add and briefly mix lubricant (e.g., magnesium stearate)
3. Compress on a tablet press

### Advantages
- **Fewer processing steps** — does not require granulation, does not require solvent, and uses less energy
- **No heat or moisture exposure** — moisture-sensitive or heat-sensitive ingredients can be used safely
- **Cost-effective** — most straightforward and least complex way to produce tablets
- **Easily scalable** for large-batch production
- **Simpler facility requirements** — no granulator or dryer needed, reducing equipment and validation costs

### Disadvantages
- **Requires critical excipient selection** — raw materials must demonstrate good flowability and compressibility
- **Content uniformity challenges** — with low-dose APIs, it may be difficult to achieve homogeneity; segregation, de-mixing, or sedimentation may occur if particle sizes differ significantly
- **Limited API range** — not all drugs have suitable powder properties for direct compression
- **Segregation risk** — during blending or hopper discharge if particle sizes differ

## Wet Granulation

### Process
Wet granulation is the most widely used oral solid dosage (OSD) method. It involves multiple steps that transform powders into granules, which are then compressed into tablets.

Steps:
1. Dry mixing of API and excipients in a high-shear mixer or planetary mixer
2. Addition of liquid binder solution (e.g., polyvinylpyrrolidone in water or ethanol) to form a wet mass
3. Wet screening through a sieve to create uniform granules
4. Drying in a fluid bed dryer or tray oven (target moisture: 1–3%)
5. Dry screening (sizing) and blending with extragranular excipients (lubricant, disintegrant)
6. Compression on a rotary tablet press

### Advantages
- **Improved flowability and compressibility** — granulation narrows particle size distribution, eliminating segregation problems
- **Superior content uniformity** — ensures homogeneous API distribution, especially important for low-dose drugs and drugs with narrow therapeutic windows
- **Reduced dust generation** — improves operator safety and reduces cross-contamination risk
- **Higher API loading possible** — permits higher quantities of API in the tablet

### Disadvantages
- **Multiple steps and costly equipment** — requires high-shear mixer, fluid bed dryer; more time-consuming and expensive
- **Heat and moisture exposure** — not suitable for APIs sensitive to moisture or heat involved in drying
- **Energy-intensive drying** — must be carefully controlled to avoid over-drying (brittle granules) or under-drying (stability issues)
- **Risk of cross-contamination and product loss** during different processing steps

## When to Choose Each Method

| Factor | Direct Compression | Wet Granulation |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| API moisture sensitivity | Preferred (no water) | Not suitable |
| API dose | Moderate to high (>25 mg) | Any, especially low-dose |
| Powder flow properties | Must be good | Can be poor |
| Content uniformity need | Less critical | Critical (low-dose) |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Industry usage | ~20% of tablets | Most widely used globally |

## Key Equipment

- **Direct Compression:** V-blender or bin blender, tablet press
- **Wet Granulation:** High-shear mixer/rapid mixer granulator, fluid bed dryer, sieve/screen, rotary tablet press
