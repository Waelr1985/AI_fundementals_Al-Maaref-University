## Overview

Warfarin is an oral anticoagulant that inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase (VKORC1), reducing the synthesis of vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, and X). It falls in the narrow therapeutic index drug category and is subject to numerous drug interactions.

## Mechanism of Interactions

Warfarin's hepatic metabolism and protein binding are the most common mechanisms for drug-drug interactions. Warfarin is metabolized via the cytochrome P450 system, primarily by:
- **CYP2C9** (metabolizes the more potent S-enantiomer)
- **CYP1A2**
- **CYP3A4**

The S-enantiomer of warfarin is 2.7 to 3.8 times more potent than the R-enantiomer. Since the S-enantiomer is primarily metabolized by CYP2C9, drug interactions affecting this pathway are the most clinically significant.

Medications with higher protein binding affinity can displace warfarin, creating more free warfarin in the bloodstream, though this mechanism is less clinically significant than enzyme inhibition.

## Drugs That Potentiate Warfarin (Increase Bleeding Risk)

### Fluconazole (and azole antifungals)
- **Mechanism:** Potent CYP2C9 inhibitor — reduces metabolism of S-warfarin
- **Effect:** Significantly elevated INR, increased bleeding risk
- **Management:** Dose reduction and close INR monitoring required

### Amiodarone
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits both CYP2C9 and CYP3A4
- **Effect:** Prolonged, significant rise in INR
- **Note:** Amiodarone has an extremely long half-life (40–55 days), so the effect persists for weeks after discontinuation
- **Management:** Warfarin dose reduction of 30–50% typically recommended

### Metronidazole
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits CYP2C9
- **Effect:** Enhanced anticoagulant activity and elevated INR
- **Management:** INR checks within 3–5 days of initiation

### Aspirin and NSAIDs
- **Mechanism:** Pharmacodynamic interaction — inhibit platelet function and irritate gastric mucosa
- **Effect:** Significantly increased risk of gastrointestinal bleeding when combined with warfarin

## Drugs That Antagonize Warfarin (Decrease Efficacy)

### Rifampin
- **Mechanism:** One of the most potent inducers of CYP2C9, CYP3A4, and CYP1A2
- **Effect:** Dramatically accelerates warfarin metabolism, reducing INR to sub-therapeutic levels
- **Management:** May require 2- to 3-fold increase in warfarin dose; frequent INR monitoring during and after therapy

### Carbamazepine
- **Mechanism:** Induces CYP3A4 and CYP2C9
- **Effect:** Decreased warfarin plasma concentration and reduced anticoagulant effect
- **Risk:** Under-anticoagulation, raising risk of thromboembolic events (DVT, stroke)

### Vitamin K (dietary or supplemental)
- **Mechanism:** Directly opposes warfarin's mechanism — replenishes vitamin K pool for clotting factor synthesis
- **Effect:** Reduces INR and diminishes therapeutic effect
- **Management:** Patients counseled to maintain consistent dietary intake of vitamin K

## INR Monitoring

- Standard test for monitoring warfarin therapy
- Therapeutic range: **2.0–3.0** for most indications (atrial fibrillation, VTE)
- Therapeutic range: **2.5–3.5** for mechanical heart valves
- INR can fluctuate due to genetics (CYP2C9, VKORC1 polymorphisms), diet, medications, and liver function
- Monitor weekly during dose adjustments, at least monthly when stable
- Recheck INR within 1–7 days when supratherapeutic or subtherapeutic
- Recheck INR when starting, discontinuing, or changing doses of interacting medications
